# Persona: PO (Product Owner — the Demand Translator)

> Second document in the persona series. Maps the PO end to end: who he is, how he reasons, what he decides, what he delivers — and the **data structure** that makes that reasoning operable on screen.
>
> If the [Submitter](./01-submitter.md) is the persona whose core is **capturing** a pain honestly, the PO is the persona whose core is **deciding** — first the demand's path, then the product's form. **Decision is first class** here, in the same way confidence is first class there.

---

## Purpose of this document

Current documents define the PO in a **fragmented and distributed** way: his position in the flow is in [`README.md`](../README.md), his handoffs are scattered across [`interactions/`](../interactions/) files (01, 02, 05, 07, 08), and the shape of his deliverable is in the Readiness Package. No single place brings this together into a unified persona view — exactly the problem the Submitter doc solved for the upstream side.

Additionally, the [unified prototype](../prototypes/demandos-prototype-unified-v1.tsx) — built by learning from the role — encoded knowledge that the docs **do not yet have**: triage as a scoreable queue with decision+rationale per item, AI pre-filled rationalization, *trace-to-source* on every RP claim, and a dashboard with *pay-justifying* KPIs (throughput, avoided delay cost, first-version acceptance).

This document **consolidates both sources**, **incorporates market calibration** (research in Cagan/SVPG, Scrum Guide 2020, Upstream Kanban, and modern PRD literature), and **corrects a structural direction** that prior docs had wrong: the relationship between the RP, the CTO's assessment, and the PRD (see §2 and §10). It is simultaneously:

- **Documentation** of the persona (evolves existing docs);
- **Specification** of the abstraction that should become screen value.

The principle that runs through everything: **decision is first class.** Every passage of a demand through the PO is a recorded, justified, and traceable decision — never an implicit note.

## 1. Who the PO is

The PO is the **translator** — the first persona whose face points **inward**, toward engineering, but who still **carries the external customer pain with him**. While the Submitter speaks the language of *problem, value, opportunity, relationship*, the PO speaks the language of *scope, context, business rule, acceptance criterion, risk* — without ever losing sight of the pain that originated everything. He receives the raw demand and transforms it into **executable product context**.

In the [`README.md`](../README.md) diagram, his role fits in one line:

> **PO — Rationalization: transforms pain into product context.**

Together with the CTO, he forms the **translation layer** — the layer that "stops merely receiving requests and starts producing context, scope, and direction". Without this layer, the README is explicit about what happens: *"Engineering becomes a help desk, the PO becomes a meeting note-taker, and the CTO becomes a firefighter."*

> **Central design constraint:** the PO is neither the originator nor the executor. He does not invent the demand (it comes from the Submitter) and does not build it (that is downstream's job). His value is entirely **product judgment** — deciding what advances, in what form, with what context, and how ready it is. Anything that reduces him to a typist (filling sections by hand, transcribing meetings) destroys the role.

In the prototype example he is **Marina Costa, PO**.

### 1.1 Calibration note — why "PO" here is not the Scrum "PO"

The product literature uses "Product Owner" in a **narrower** sense than this project. It is worth making the divergence explicit so as not to confuse a reader familiar with the market:

- In the **Scrum Guide (2020)**, the PO is *accountable for maximizing product value*, and his formal core is "developing and communicating the Product Goal" and managing the Product Backlog. It is a **value accountability**, not a job title.
- In **Cagan / SVPG**, "Product Owner" is described as *merely a backlog administration role* within the agile team — a **small subset** of the Product Manager's responsibilities, who is the one who actually understands customers and ensures value + feasibility.

**The "PO" in this project is deliberately broader than both.** He performs strategic triage, deep rationalization of pain, and produces the readiness document. In market vocabulary, he is closer to the **Product Manager / Value Maximizer** than to the backlog-administrator PO.

The choice of the term is intentional and has a thesis behind it: **"owner" carries ownership and accountability over the *product* — the synergy with the external customer pain** — while "manager" tends, in practice, to manage the *delivery form*, pointing inward. In this model, the person who rationalizes pain needs to be closest to the external world; the one pointing more inward (the PM, downstream) handles tangibilization. That is why the role that holds the product vision is called **owner**, not manager.

> In small teams, the function can be accumulated (PM, Product Ops, Chief of Staff, Founder Associate) — but it must exist. It is what Goldratt calls "elevating the constraint" and what Perri & Tilles describe as Product Operations (see [`references.md`](../references.md)).

## 2. The artifact chain — where the PO starts and ends

This is the most important **structural correction** relative to prior docs. The PO's deliverable does **not** go directly to the PM, and the RP is **not** a co-edited package with the CTO. The correct chain is:

```
Intake Record         →   Readiness Package (RP)   →   PRD                      →   downstream
(Submitter, alone)        (PO, alone)                  (RP + Technical Assessment)   (PM receives)
                                                        └── PO + CTO merge ──┘
```

| Artifact | Author | What it is | Who receives it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Submitter Brief** | Submitter | The pain captured honestly (per-field confidence) | PO |
| **Intake Record** | **PO** (act 1 — triage) | Formalizes the brief, assigns the `INT` ID, and records the routing decision | rationalization |
| **Readiness Package (RP)** | **PO, alone** (act 2 — rationalization) | The **product readiness definition** — vision, problem, scope, rules, criteria, metrics. A **complete, self-sufficient** document | is merged into the PRD |
| **Technical Assessment** | **CTO, alone** | Feasibility, constraints, architecture, technical risks, ADRs | is merged into the PRD |
| **PRD** | PO + CTO (merge) | **RP + Technical Assessment combined** — the document that opens downstream | **PM** |

Three consequences that change the existing docs:

1. **The RP is the end of the PO's product work** — it is his *definition of ready*, a product vision document that stands on its own. It has no sections authored by the CTO inside it.
2. **The CTO produces his own artifact** (the Technical Assessment), separate, parallel. The CTO does not co-edit the RP; he responds to it.
3. **The PRD is the merge of both**, and it is the **PRD** — not the RP — that is delivered to the PM and initiates downstream. [`interactions/07-po-to-pm.md`](../interactions/07-po-to-pm.md) already reflects this: the artifact transferred to the PM is the **PRD**, not the RP.

> **Why separate.** Modern PRD literature and this project's upstream rule point in the same direction: a product document **captures validated product decisions and does not dictate implementation** — it can reference technical feasibility, but does not own it. Mixing product authorship and technical authorship in the same document is what produces the "PO as meeting note-taker" and the "CTO who rewrites the product". Keeping them as two artifacts that *merge* into the PRD preserves each author's ownership and makes every claim traceable to its author.

## 3. The PO's two acts — triage and rationalization

The PO's work is **not a continuous flow**. It consists of **two distinct acts**, with different natures, separated by a gate. Confusing them is the classic design error (the demand "enters rationalization" before being judged worthy of it).

| Act | Nature | The question | The output | Renders on screen as |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Triage** | **Routing** decision · fast · disposable | "Does this demand deserve our effort — and by which path?" | One of 4 decisions + rationale | **Scoreable queue** — items to evaluate, coverage score, path decision |
| **Rationalization** | **Construction** decision · deep · cumulative | "Given it advances, what is the product form of this pain?" | Frozen **RP** (readiness definition) | **Readiness canvas** — filled sections, per-section confidence, freeze gate |

> **The line separating the two acts is the `Product Ready` state.** Triage only ends in a way that opens rationalization when the decision is *Product Ready*. The other three decisions (Discovery, Backlog, Reject) **close** the PO's current pass on the demand — the demand exits through a side door, not into the RP.

This separation is the direct application of **Stage-Gate** (Cooper): triage is the *gate decision*; rationalization is the *stage* of work that only runs after a go. And the frozen RP is the Upstream Kanban **commitment point** — the point at which "the work is unambiguous, well-defined, and the business confirms it understands the risks and is willing to invest".

## 4. The decision model (core)

Decision is the axis that sustains everything else — the exact analogue to the Submitter's trust model. Every PO decision, in both acts, carries:

| Attribute | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `verdict` | the choice itself (e.g.: `Product Ready`, or "include in scope", or "escalate to CTO") |
| `rationale` | **why** — text that makes the decision defensible, never optional |
| `basis` | what evidence it relies on (intake record, Submitter disposition, ADR, portfolio data) |
| `source` | evidence traceability (*trace-to-source* — inherited from the Submitter's honesty layer) |
| `reversible` | does the decision close a door (Reject) or open a recoverable route (Discovery)? |

This is the **defensibility layer**: the PRD, the PM, and stakeholders (CFO, CEO) do not just receive "the demand advanced" — they receive *why* it advanced, *in what form*, and *based on what*, with every claim traceable to its origin.

> The Submitter taught us that confidence is not a generic number: it is **per field**. The PO extends the same logic to decisions: defensibility is not a rubber stamp at the end — it is **per decision and per claim**. That granularity is the main new contribution the prototype taught us about the PO.

## 5. Data structure — deterministic in form, non-deterministic in content

The guiding phrase, identical to the Submitter's: **the structure is fixed; the content varies.** The data model is known in advance; what fills each field (and which triage items or sections emerge) is generated from the content of each demand.

```
— ACT 1: TRIAGE —
TriageCriterion        (DETERMINISTIC — the fixed routing checklist)
  id                stable key
  label             "Is it a real problem?" · "Is it recurring?" · "Fits the vision?"
  dimension         RealProblem | Recurrence | StrategicFit | BusinessImpact | TechSignal | Urgency
  why               why routing requires evaluating this
  isPath            true = this is the item that carries the final PATH DECISION

TriageAssessment       (NON-DETERMINISTIC content, deterministic envelope)
  criterionId       → which criterion this assessment answers
  verdict           the decision for this item (on isPath: Product Ready | Discovery | Backlog | Reject)
  rationale         why — required
  basis / source    evidence + traceability (trace-to-source)

— ACT 2: RATIONALIZATION (produces the RP) —
RPSection              (DETERMINISTIC — the sections of the readiness definition; see §6.2)
  id                stable key
  title             "Context and Problem" · "Scope" · "Non-Functional Requirements" · ...
  group             Context | Scope | Behavior | Quality | Success | Risks | Roadmap
  why               what this section guarantees to whoever receives it
  satisfiedWhen     rubric: what is "good enough" (guides AI judgment)
  weight            contribution to the RP readiness score
  blocksFreeze      true = RP does not freeze until this section is resolved

RPEntry                (NON-DETERMINISTIC content, deterministic envelope)
  sectionId         → which section this entry fills
  content           the product text
  confidence        0–100, evaluated against satisfiedWhen  (reuses the Submitter's layer)
  source            origin — inherited from intake, AI-inferred, or PO-authored
  status            empty | low_confidence | resolved
  origin            inherited | ai_drafted | po_authored | reused_from_KB
  disposition       decided | inherited | ai_drafted | discovery   (see §7)

TechAssessmentRef      (BRIDGE to the CTO's artifact — NOT RP content)
  status            not_requested | requested | in_progress | signed | vetoed
  verdict           feasible | feasible-with-caveats | infeasible-as-scoped
  link              reference to the Technical Assessment (separate CTO artifact)
  → the RP REFERENCES this; it does not absorb it. The merge happens in the PRD.

— DERIVED, regenerated on every change (the engine, not stored truth) —
  triageScore       = % of criteria assessed   ← gate for "can conclude triage"
  rpReadiness       = f(weights, statuses)        (low_confidence counts as partial)
  freezeReady       = all sections with blocksFreeze resolved
                      AND TechAssessmentRef.status ∈ {signed, not_requested}
                      (does not freeze with pending assessment when one was requested)
```

The clean line: **`TriageCriterion` and `RPSection` are the deterministic contracts; everything below `…Entry.content` is generated; `triageScore` / `rpReadiness` / `freezeReady` are pure functions of what is above.** It is the **same engine** as the Submitter's (fixed requirement → graded entry → derived score/gate), instantiated with the PO's requirements. `TechAssessmentRef` is the new piece: a **traceable bridge** to the CTO's artifact, not a co-edited section.

## 6. Compliance requirements — the PO's two contracts

The PO has **two contracts**, one per act.

### 6.1 Triage contract — the routing criteria

Derived from the "2 — Initial Triage" step in [`README.md`](../README.md). Each criterion is a judgment question; the `isPath` item is the one that carries the final decision.

| # | Criterion (`label`) | Dimension | Assessed when… | Is the path? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Is it a real problem (not an isolated symptom)? | RealProblem | There is an observable pattern, not a single incident | — |
| 2 | Is it recurring / does it have volume? | Recurrence | The pain reappears or affects many | — |
| 3 | Fits the product vision? | StrategicFit | Aligned with strategy, not an opportunistic detour | — |
| 4 | What is the technical and business impact? | BusinessImpact | Magnitude and direction of value are clear | — |
| 5 | Does urgency and impact justify now? | Urgency | "Why now" and the cost of waiting | — |
| 6 | **Path decision** | Routing | All previous items assessed | ✅ |

The **path decision** (`isPath`) has exactly four values, inherited from [`interactions/01-sales-to-po.md`](../interactions/01-sales-to-po.md):

| Decision | What it means | Effect on demand state | Door |
|---|---|---|---|
| `Product Ready` | Clear context, no blocking unknowns | → **In Rationalization** | opens Act 2 |
| `Discovery` | Investigate before rationalizing (time-box, e.g.: 2 weeks) | → **In Discovery** | side door, recoverable |
| `Backlog` | Good demand, but not a priority now | → **Backlog** (Opportunity) | side door, recoverable |
| `Reject` | Outside strategy or low value | → **Rejected** | closes (with rationale) |

> **Triage gate:** `triageScore = 100%` (every criterion assessed) to *be able to conclude*. This does not force a specific decision — it forces the decision to be **informed** (no criterion was skipped). The Submitter has been notified of the result, whatever it is.

### 6.2 Rationalization contract — the RP readiness definition

Here is the **enrichment** the research called for. The old set of 12 sections treated "Integrations" and "Technical/Architectural Impact" as RP sections (authored by the CTO). With the new chain (§2), **those move out of the RP and become the CTO's Technical Assessment**; the RP references the verdict via `TechAssessmentRef`. In exchange, the RP **gains** the sections that modern PRD literature identifies as most frequently absent — and that are pure product territory: non-functional requirements, metrics with guardrails, and edge cases as first class.

The RP readiness definition (PO's sole authorship):

| # | Section | Group | Blocks freeze? | Origin note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Executive Summary | Context | ✅ | inherited |
| 2 | Context and Problem (the pain, not the solution) | Context | ✅ | inherited — golden rule |
| 3 | Objectives and Expected Outcome | Context | ✅ | inherited |
| 4 | Impacted Personas / Jobs-to-be-done | Context | ✅ | inherited (was §5) |
| 5 | Included and Excluded Scope | Scope | ✅ | inherited — protects downstream from scope creep |
| 6 | Business Rules and Flows | Scope | ✅ | inherited |
| 7 | **User Stories + Acceptance Criteria** | Behavior | ✅ | **new/explicit** — "As X, I want Y, so that Z"; acceptance in Given/When/Then, verifiable by non-dev, with specific limits |
| 8 | **Non-Functional Requirements (NFRs)** | Quality | ✅ | **new** — performance, reliability, security, usability, compatibility, maintainability (ISO/IEC 25010-style checklist); the #1 gap that causes rework |
| 9 | **Edge Cases and Failure Modes** | Quality | ✅ | **new/explicit** — error states, timeouts, permissions; for AI features, model behavior and low-confidence handling |
| 10 | **Success Metrics (primary · secondary · guardrail)** | Success | ✅ | **enriched** — table with measurement timeline; leading and lagging indicators |
| 11 | Success and Acceptance Criteria (for the release) | Success | ✅ | inherited (was §11) |
| 12 | Risks and Dependencies (product/business) | Risks | ✅ | inherited — **technical** risks migrate to the Technical Assessment |
| 13 | Preliminary Effort and Cost Assessment | Risks | — | inherited (was §10); the firm number comes from the CTO in the Assessment |
| 14 | Suggested Roadmap | Roadmap | — | inherited (was §12) |
| — | **Technical Assessment Reference** | (bridge) | ✅ if requested | `TechAssessmentRef` — verdict + link, not content |

> **Golden rule inherited:** *problem before solution.* If section 2 describes a solution instead of the problem, it is not satisfied — the same principle the Submitter applies to her requirement 1. The PO is the guardian of this rule downward, just as the intake applies it upward. And consistent with the literature: the RP **captures validated product decisions and does not dictate technical implementation**.

> **Scales with complexity.** For a small improvement, the RP compresses to its spine: Problem → Target metric → Scope (in/out) → 3-5 acceptance criteria → NFRs (only those at risk). The contract is the ceiling, not the mandatory floor — consistent with the project's anti-bureaucracy principle ("when it becomes bureaucracy, simplify").

The content of each section is non-deterministic (varies by demand). The **existence and shape** of the sections is deterministic.

## 7. The decisions that guide the work — and why "not deciding yet" is valid

The PO's unit of work is **the decision** (just as the Submitter's unit is the question). Each decision:

- targets a triage criterion **or** an RP section;
- is made in **product language** (scope, rule, risk, criterion, metric);
- carries a required `rationale` — it exists to be **defensible**, not just recorded.

### "I cannot decide yet" is not paralysis — it is a structured disposition

Just as the Submitter's "I don't know" never blocks, the PO's uncertainty never stalls the flow. A section or criterion has **several paths to "resolved enough to advance"**:

| Disposition | What it means | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| `decided` | The PO decides directly | full confidence |
| `inherited` | Comes ready from the Submitter's intake (with `source`) | partial confidence, traceable |
| `ai_drafted` | The AI pre-filled it; the PO reviews and confirms | partial confidence until confirmed |
| `discovery` | The product unknown requires investigation before deciding | counts as *resolved-as-unknown*, time-boxed |

> Note that `escalated` **was removed** from the RP disposition list. Escalating to the CTO is no longer "a section another person fills inside the RP" — it is a **dependency on another artifact** (the Technical Assessment), modeled as `TechAssessmentRef`. That is the data-level translation of the structural correction in §2.

The gate is **not** "the PO knows everything". The gate is **"every blocking section has an honest disposition, and the Technical Assessment has returned signed when it was requested"**. This keeps the screen as a **judgment workspace**, not a form: the AI brings the draft, the PO exercises judgment, and every decision is defensible.

## 8. Indicators — the mirror that governs the queue (not a single RP)

The Submitter has RICE-lite as a mirror that sharpens *one* demand. The PO works in **portfolio** — his productive tension is not within one demand, it is **between demands in the queue**. The indicators that matter:

| Indicator | The PO's question | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Delay cost** | "What do we lose per day while this waits?" ($/day) | the sequencing signal — inherited from Reinertsen |
| **SLA remaining** | "How long until the triage commitment is breached?" | the queue pressure |
| **Estimated effort** | "How expensive is it to rationalize + execute?" | preliminary in the RP, firmed by the CTO in the Assessment |
| **Possible reuse** | "Have we decided something similar? Is there a reusable ADR/RP?" | the velocity lever |

### The "challenge sequencing" mechanism

As with the Submitter, the value is not in the numbers — it is in the **tension between them**, as a provocation:

- **High delay cost + high effort** → *"Expensive to wait and expensive to do — is this next, or does it need slicing?"*
- **SLA about to breach + low impact** → *"About to breach the SLA, but is it worth the effort, or is it an honest Backlog candidate?"*
- **New demand very similar to a recent RP** → *"We already rationalized something like this — reuse the context instead of starting over?"*

Each provocation that sharpens sequencing improves throughput — mirror and gate pull in the same direction, exactly as on the Submitter's side.

## 9. How the PO is measured — portfolio view

The prototype gave the PO a dashboard the docs never imagined. These metrics *are* how he reasons about his own work — and they are **pay-justifying** (justify paying for the platform):

| Metric | What it says | Origin |
|---|---|---|
| **Throughput of frozen RPs** (e.g.: 9/week) | How much executable context he produces — the Hero Metric | prototype KPI |
| **Avoided delay cost** (e.g.: $47k/month) | Value preserved by respecting SLAs | prototype drill-down |
| **PM first-version acceptance** (e.g.: 89% vs 64% before) | PRD quality — how many pass without being returned | prototype KPI |
| **Average time in rationalization** (e.g.: 2.4 days, −1.8d with AI) | Translation speed | prototype KPI |
| **PRDs returned by PM** (e.g.: 11%) | The counter-indicator — what was returned and why | prototype KPI |
| **AI-saved hours** (rationalization: ~108h → ~21h / 18 demands) | The work AI did instead of him | AI Impact Meter |

> **Silent insight:** "PM first-version acceptance" is the PO's mirror on himself — the direct analogue to the Submitter's "first-version acceptance". It tells whether he is feeding downstream well, without having to audit every PRD. The rise from 64% → 89% is the platform's value thesis for this role.

## 10. The lateral relationship with the CTO — two artifacts that merge

The PO is the only upstream role with a **lateral interaction** within the same layer: escalation to the CTO (see [`interactions/05-po-to-cto.md`](../interactions/05-po-to-cto.md)). With the new chain from §2, this relationship changes shape and deserves to be in the persona:

- The PO **escalates** when the demand touches infrastructure, platform, multi-tenancy, AI/runtime, security, or integrations with unknowns.
- The PO delivers to the CTO the **RP** (the product vision) + specific questions. **Not** "empty sections for the CTO to fill in".
- The CTO produces his own **artifact — the Technical Assessment** — with feasibility, constraints, architecture, technical risks, and ADRs. The CTO **never** edits the RP.
- The CTO can **veto** feasibility; in that case the PO revises the RP's scope — but the CTO does not redefine the product.
- **The merge happens in the PRD:** `PRD = RP (PO) + Technical Assessment (CTO)`. It is the PRD that goes to the PM.

In the data model this is the `TechAssessmentRef` in §5: the RP *references* the assessment (status + verdict + link), it does not absorb it. In the prototype, the corresponding WOW moment is that the AI already arrives with **suggested ADRs** (and reused ADRs from the base) — the CTO approves/adjusts instead of writing from scratch, and the result goes into the Assessment, not the RP.

> **Update already applied in docs:** [`interactions/05-po-to-cto.md`](../interactions/05-po-to-cto.md) describes the CTO producing the **Technical Assessment** as a separate artifact (no longer "Section 8 and additions to Sections 7 and 9" of the RP — the CTO does not edit the RP), and the artifact index in [`README.md` §12](../README.md) has dedicated rows for the **Technical Assessment** and the **PRD**.

## 11. The deliverable and the handoff

- **Product deliverable:** the **Readiness Package** (`RP-YYYY-NNN`) — the frozen product readiness definition, authored solely by the PO.
- **Combined deliverable:** the **PRD** (`PRD-YYYY-NNN`) — `RP + Technical Assessment`. It is what opens downstream.
- **RP freeze gate:** `freezeReady = true` (every `blocksFreeze` section resolved and, if the CTO was requested, `TechAssessmentRef.status = signed`). It is the Stage-Gate *gate decision* and the Upstream Kanban *commitment point* turned into a number.
- **Handoff:** the **PRD** is delivered to the **PM** (downstream). The PM has **explicit authority to reject** and return it with specific gaps (see [`interactions/07-po-to-pm.md`](../interactions/07-po-to-pm.md), to be updated from "RP" to "PRD"); in that case the PO (or the CTO, depending on the gap) addresses only the gaps and increments the version. The PO also **receives back** from the PM at two moments: capacity escalation ([`08`](../interactions/08-pm-to-po-capacity.md)) and feedback loop closure.

> After the handoff, the PO **no longer drives the demand day to day** — execution belongs to the PM. But the PO owns the **Product Backlog** (epics, stories, acceptance criteria) and is the point where the feedback loop closes to update priorities.

## 12. Screen value

The PO's screen should **not** feel like a form of N sections being filled in by hand. It should feel like the system **has already rationalized the demand** and is asking for the PO's **judgment** on what it produced.

- **Triage** renders as *routing decision*: the queue, the criteria to judge, the path — fast, disposable, with rationale.
- **Rationalization** renders as *materialized product vision*: the RP sections pre-filled by AI, each with confidence and origin, each claim traceable (*trace-to-source*) — and the PO's work is to review, edit, justify, and freeze.
- **The PRD merge** renders as *visible stitching*: the PO's RP alongside the CTO's Technical Assessment, forming the PRD — each half with its clear author, without one writing over the other.

The WOW moment is the opposite of the README's fear: instead of "meeting note-taker", the PO arrives at a demand where the AI **has already read the intake, already suggested the scope, already drafted user stories and NFRs, already found reusable ADRs** — and his time becomes high-value judgment, not typing. Rationalization dropping from ~108h to ~21h across 18 demands *is* that WOW, quantified.

## 13. What the prototype and research taught us (and the docs did not yet have)

1. **Per-item decision with rationale** (`verdict / rationale / basis / source`) — the defensibility layer, analogous to the Submitter's honesty layer.
2. **Triage as a scoreable queue** — the Stage-Gate routing gate turned into `triageScore` + path decision with 4 values.
3. **AI pre-filled rationalization** — the system drafts the sections and the PO judges ("you only review, edit, and justify").
4. **Trace-to-source in the RP** — every claim carries provenance, making the package defensible to PM, CFO, and CEO.
5. **The RP → PRD → PM chain** — the RP is the PO's readiness definition; the PRD is the merge with the CTO; it is the PRD that opens downstream. *(project direction correction)*
6. **Enriched RP** — user stories with testable acceptance (Given/When/Then), explicit NFRs (ISO/IEC 25010), edge cases/failure modes as first class, metrics with guardrails. *(market research)*
7. **Market calibration** — "PO" here is deliberately the Value Maximizer/PM in the Cagan/Scrum sense, not the backlog-administrator PO. *(market research)*
8. **Pay-justifying KPIs** — throughput, avoided delay cost, first-version acceptance (64% → 89%), PRDs returned.
